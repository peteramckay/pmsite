Much Faster Beams

By Peter A. McKay | May 21,2015 | Tags: Company News, Beams, HTTPS, Server Performance

When we upgraded our open Beams software to support secure HTTPS browsing by default for news sites recently, we by no means intended to take our eye off the ball regarding other stuff publishers require, especially server speed.

Today we're shifting focus a bit, back toward raw performance, with release of the Beams v0.2.5 update, now available for free download. Thanks to improved caching and a few other tweaks, it's 44% faster than our last release, with the same HTTPS support as ever.

We've also done benchmark testing of this site, running Beams v0.2.5, versus a cohort of major third-party news sites. We'd say the Beams server's performance stacked up very well, while offering greater user privacy than the vast majority of the big boys, including the New York Times, Washington Post, L.A. Times, Wall Street Journal, and others.

More generally — and to put the chest-thumping aside for a moment — we hope this little experiment will at least provide valuable proof of concept to the news industry that switching to HTTPS is doable without compromising performance — a complaint sometimes raised in the past by HTTPS critics. If anything, doing such an upgrade can be an opportunity to do overdue upgrades in other areas as well, thus boosting overall site performance.

OK. Enough of that. Back to the chest-thumping.

Using Apache Bench running on a cloud-based server, we pinged this site (roscoelabs.com) both before and after upgrading to Beams v0.2.5, with a simple test of 1,000 HTTP requests, running at a maximum concurrency of 200 at a time. We then visited 20 popular English-language news sites, noted their canonical urls that printed in our browser address bar, and tested those urls using the same parameters.

You'll note that Beams wasn't the fastest, period. But it was easily the fastest secure server built for news, which is precisely where we want to be. It's the best of both worlds — performance and user privacy.

Our 3.15-second test result was also good enough to beat several large orgs even when they were running vanilla HTTP, which should've put them at a natural advantage according to the old conventional wisdom.

So much for that.

Peter McKay is co-founder and chief product officer of Roscoe Labs.
